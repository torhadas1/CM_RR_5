Research Information

The Kebos Woodlands was once a thriving ecosystem, home to various kinds of exotic fauna and flora not found anywhere else in the world. However, in recent years, development of local areas for farmlands and roads, overgrazing by farm animals, and illegal deforestation have caused a sharp reduction in local biomass (defined as the surface coverage of fauna like trees, bushes, and grass). This has had a knock-on effect on the animal species that live in the Woodlands, throwing the ecosystem in chaos.

Your team of conservationist have been approached by the governing body of the Woodlands to help restore the Kebos Woodlands to its former glory.

Objective

Determine if the proposed initiatives can achieve a 20% increase in total biomass units (defined as a km² of fauna) in the Varla Valley and Custodia Pass.

Various brainstorming sessions and feasibility studies were also conducted, resulting in the following initiatives to be implemented in the coming months:

Initiative 1:
Transplant 30% of the trees from Oakwood to Varla Valley

Only trees that are
at least 3-years old
would be transplanted to minimize loss during transplantation. Unfortunately, transplantation is a difficult process and even with the precautions,
1m² of trees are lost for every 7m² transplanted
.

Transplanting trees in the new location will also damage local flora, with an expected loss of
1m² of bushes for every 20m² of trees successfully transplanted
and
1m² of grass for every 10m² of trees successfully transplanted
.

Initiative 2:
Set up animal barriers in Varla Valley, Riverbend, and Custodia Pass

Animal barriers will prevent overgrazing in the select zones, helping restore bush and grass biomass over time. A
5% recovery is expected for bushes, independent of other initiatives.
Meanwhile, a
10% recovery is expected for grass, independent of other initiatives.
